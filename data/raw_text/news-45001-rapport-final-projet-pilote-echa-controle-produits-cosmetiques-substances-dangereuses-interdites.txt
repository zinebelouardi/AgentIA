1. Executive summary 
The European Chemicals Agency’s Forum for Exchange of Information on Enforcement (Forum) 
has finalised its pilot project that focused on the enforcement of PFCAs and related substances, 
including PFOA and D4/D5 mainly in cosmetic products. 
The project contributes to the harmonised enforcement of integrated control of chemicals in 
cosmetic products with respect to: 
a. POPs Regulation (Annex I restrictions) 
b. REACH Regulation (Annex XVII restrictions) 
This was an EU enforcement project inspecting 4686 products between November 2023 and 
April 2024 in 13 Member States1. 
The overall non-compliance rate was 6.1%. Most of the inspections were on cosmetic products 
which had a non-compliance rate of 6.4%. The inspections were mainly done by checking the 
ingredients list online or on the packaging for INCI names of the restricted substances. The 
most non-compliant restricted substances identified were perfluorononyl dimethicone and 
cyclopentasiloxane (D5) (in wash-off cosmetic products).

2. Introduction 
The Forum pilot project was carried out with the aim of enforcement on restricted substances 
under both the POPs and REACH Regulations mainly in cosmetic products2 but also in other 
products. 
The project covered: 
• 
Enforcement of POPs Annex I restrictions on PFOA3 in cosmetic products, 
• 
Enforcement of REACH Annex XVII PFCAs (entry 68)4 and D4/D5 (entry 70) restrictions 
applicable to cosmetic products. The entry 70 restricts D4 and D5 in wash-off 
cosmetics. 
Main target were companies that import or formulate cosmetic products to be sold in the EEA. 
Distributors of cosmetic products were also in the scope of this pilot project. 
The project was focused on consumer products (cosmetics) but also covered products for 
professional use. 
Every NEA was free to select the cosmetic products they wished to check within the project for 
a different restricted substance and the associated legal requirements. The check was mainly 
done based on the ingredients labelled on the cosmetic products themselves or on the website 
of the selected product. Another option was to use laboratory tests to check for the presence 
of the restricted substances. 
Even if the focus of the project was on cosmetic products, it was up to the participating 
country to decide if they wanted to check and report these restricted substances in any other 
mixture or article. 
The operational phase of the project ran from November 2023 to April 2024. The participating 
countries were supported by the Forum Working Group.

3. Results 
3.1. General overview 
The Forum has finalised its pilot project that focused on the enforcement of restricted 
substances under both the POPs and REACH Regulations mainly in relation to cosmetic 
products. For the POPs Regulation, the checks focused on PFOA, its salts, and related 
substances as restricted under Annex I. Under the REACH Regulation, the checks targeted C9-
C14 PFCAs, their salts, and related substances restricted under entry 68, as well as D4 and D5 
restricted under entry 70. The project covered 13 participating countries encompassing 4686 
checked products from 345 companies. Of these, 4478 were cosmetic products and 208 were 
other types such as detergents and cleaning products. 
A total of 285 non-compliant products were reported in the project5, resulting in a non-
compliance rate of 6.1% from all inspected products (cosmetic and other products). 
All the non-compliances were found in cosmetic products (4478), accounting for a non-
compliance rate of 6.4% in this category of products. In the project, the reporting was done 
per product, which, for example, could include several shades or colour variants of a product 
(all different shades/colours of a product were counted as one single product). Of the 4478 
cosmetic products inspected, 4434 were checked for restricted substances under REACH and 
3761 under POPs (most of the products were checked for potential non-compliance with both, 
POPs and REACH). 
The following chapters present the detailed results of the project. 
Since no non-compliance was found in product categories other than cosmetics the project 
report focuses on cosmetic products. 
3.2. Participating countries 
A total of 13 countries participated in the pilot project (Austria, Denmark, Germany, Finland, 
Iceland, Italy, Lichtenstein, Lithuania, Luxembourg, Malta, Norway, Romania, Sweden). 
The non-compliance rate found in the different MS varied greatly, between 0 and 52.6%. This 
difference can be attributed to the different methodologies used by the MS for identifying 
products to check. As an example, one MS checked 54% of all cosmetic products inspected in 
this project and found 3.5% of the products non-compliant while another MS checked just 15% 
and found 60.4% of them to be non-compliant. 
Another aspect affecting the results might be the influence of campaigns by other institutions 
e.g. NGOs which were running prior to or in parallel with the pilot project. 
To identify the restricted substances different approaches were also possible, with the most 
common being checking the ingredients listed on the label of the product as well as on the 
company’s website.

3.5. Non-compliant substances 
A total of six different restricted substances were found in 285 non-compliant products. Table 4 
below shows the substances identified in the project and the number of non-compliant 
products containing these substances. A total of 9 products contained more than one restricted 
substance (e.g. D5 together with D4). The most frequently found substances were

Perfluorononyl dimethicone is covered by both the PFOA restriction in POPs and the C9-C14 
PFCAs restriction in REACH. Perfluorononyl dimethicone is a siloxane polymer having a C9-
perfluorinated side-chain. Based on its chemical structure, the substance is a PFOA- and C9-
C14 PFCA- related substance according to the chemical definitions in the corresponding 
restrictions. 
Cyclopentasiloxane (D5) is covered by restriction entry 70 of Annex XVII of REACH for D4 and

3.6.2. Enforcement measures and follow-up activities 
During inspections a total of 285 non-compliant products were detected. For most of these 
products enforcement measures were imposed by the enforcement authorities. It is important 
to clarify that multiple measures per case could have been taken by the enforcement 
authorities. The most applied enforcement measure was the issuance of written advice (in 80% 
of the cases where breaches were noticed). This suggests an approach by authorities in 
guiding duty holders to compliance without resorting to more severe punitive actions, or 
voluntarily actions were taken by the companies themselves (i.e. effective enforcement). See 
Table 6 below for an overview of the enforcement measures applied. 
Enforcement authorities issued administrative orders in 6% of the non-compliant cases, 
implying that more formal and legally binding actions were required to address non-
compliance. Examples of other measures taken by the authorities were – examples given – 
forwarding the case to the member state of the producer, conducting chemical analysis to 
determine limit values or forwarding the case internally to another federal enforcement 
agency.

4. Discussion, conclusions and recommendations 
Based on the analysis of the data received, the following conclusions and recommendations can 
be drawn from the project. 
4.1. Discussion and conclusions 
The POPs and REACH restrictions on PFCAs that were checked in this project cover PFOA, C9-
C14 PFCAs, their salts and related substances. The corresponding restriction entries define C9-
C14 PFCA and PFOA related substances as substances that, based on their molecular structure, 
are considered to have the potential to degrade or be transformed to C9-C14 PFCAs or PFOA. 
Therefore, hundreds of chemicals are covered by the scope of the restrictions, and only non-
exhaustive lists of the restricted substances are available (based on EC or CAS numbers). 
 
The check of restricted groups of substances, which are based on their chemical structure, 
imposes a high workload for both enforcement as well as the implementation of the obligations 
by industry. 
Enforcement issues for per- and polyfluorinated restricted substances identified in this pilot 
project were: 
- 
Regarding borderline cases the proper assignment based on the INCI names originating 
from Cosmetics Regulation to REACH related substance identifiers (CAS/EC numbers) 
demonstrates a challenge. 
- 
The conversion of the substance name to its chemical structure requires detailed 
information and chemical understanding in order to enforce the restrictions. These 
resources may be limited within the NEAs. 
- 
In some cases it is not possible to know whether the substance is restricted only based 
on the INCI name. 
- 
Some companies were not aware that the substances in their products were restricted. 
- 
Some of the related substances fall under both restrictions, which may complicate 
understanding (e.g. different given limit values). 
 
Enforcement issues for D4 and D5 restricted substances identified in this pilot project were: 
- 
In contrast to REACH restrictions D4 is prohibited under the Cosmetics Regulation, but 
D5 is not, which may complicate understanding. 
- 
D4 and D5 are only restricted in wash-off cosmetic products, but there is not a clear 
definition of wash-off (e.g. according to use description the same product can be 
compliant or non-compliant). 
 
Additionally, the definition of placing on the market differs between the regulations 
(POPs/REACH and Cosmetics). In POPs and REACH Regulations the restriction applies at all 
levels of the supply chain after the Regulation is implemented compared to the Cosmetics 
Regulation where prohibited substances can be sold if the product was placed on the market 
before the ban came into place. 
 
The pilot project helped tackle these issues (e.g. the creation of an internal substance lists 
based on the INCI names) with a dedicated group of experts helping the NEAs with the 
preparatory work aiming at efficient and harmonised enforcement. 
 
The non-compliance level of 6.4% is comparable with the REF-10 project results where the 
non-compliance level for mixtures was 9%12. It is important to highlight that, in many cases,

4.2. Recommendations 
Based on the findings of the project and the conclusions drawn, the following recommendations 
are addressed to the different actors with certain levels of responsibility in the implementation 
of the legislation related to this project (industry/duty holders, consumers, the European 
Commission, the inspectors and the ECHA Forum). 
4.2.1. Recommendations to industry/duty holders. 
1. Industry, economic actors and responsible persons for cosmetic products should pay 
attention and help raise the awareness of the members that import, manufacture or sell 
cosmetic products that they have to follow not only the provisions of the Cosmetics 
regulation but also those of POPs and REACH. Current regulatory developments 
concerning substance classes (i.e. per- and polyfluorinated compounds and D4/D5/D6) 
should be followed carefully. 
2. Industry should use substance identifiers (e.g. CAS, EC) that reflect accurately the 
composition of their manufactured substances. This is especially relevant for UVCBs. 
3. Formulators of cosmetic products are advised to carefully check Section 15 of the SDS 
regarding the presence of restricted substances. 
4. Companies selling cosmetic products are recommended to use ECHA CHEM to help 
them identifying whether their products contain restricted substances. 
5. Industry should be aware that the definition of placing on the market differs between 
the regulations (POPs/REACH and Cosmetics). 
4.2.2. Recommendations to consumers 
1. Publicly available databases may be a source to find products that contain the relevant 
restricted substances. However, it is important that these databases are up-to-date. 
2. Consumers are advised to check the list of ingredients on product labels. When checking 
the ingredients list consumers should focus on checking for Perfluorononyl dimethicone 
and Cyclopentasiloxane (D5) in wash-off cosmetic products (the highest non-compliance 
rate was found for these two substances in this project). 
3. Consumers should be aware that the restricted substances were found in different types 
of cosmetic products, sellers and any price range. 
4.2.3. Recommendations to the European Commission 
1. Regarding structure-based group restrictions it will be necessary to provide further 
assistance to enforcement as well as duty holders in terms of which substances are 
covered by the restriction. This may be achieved by guidance, demonstrative substance 
lists or similar supporting documents. 
2. To enable efficient enforcement in all MSs it is recommended to establish sufficient testing 
facilities within the European Union such as a European Testing Facility with the capability 
of conducting analyses for restricted per- and polyfluorinated substances.

4.2.4. Recommendations to the inspectors 
1. Continue enforcing restricted substances in cosmetic products, especially when new 
restrictions come into effect such as the restriction for D4/D5/D6. 
2. Cooperate with other authorities responsible for the Cosmetics Regulation when 
inspecting borderline products. 
3. Regarding notes on compliance, it is important to point out, that this only refers to 
POPs/REACH compliance. Other regulations may be applicable and the product can be 
found non-compliant by other authorities. 
4. The exchange of cases between MSs may be of high importance. The use of the ICSMS 
system is highly recommended especially for non-compliant cases. 
5. Regarding notes on non-compliance, it is important to point out that the POPs/REACH 
restrictions apply on all levels on the supply chain from the time when the restriction 
becomes mandatory (consequence: immediate withdrawal of the product). 
6. Proper preparation of enforcement actions is highly recommended (e.g. the creation of 
an internal substance list). 
4.2.5. Recommendations to the ECHA Forum 
1. Consider running a new project on cosmetic products checking per- and polyfluorinated 
compounds and D4, D5 and D6 especially when new restrictions come into force. 
2. Consider developing enforcement practices for structure-based restrictions and provide 
training for inspectors of the MSs. 
3. To exchange practices, substances lists and concerted actions may support local NEAs in 
enforcing these kinds of restrictions.
