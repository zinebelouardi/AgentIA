Abstract
Background: Individual B vitamins have many favorable effects on the skin and are 
common cosmetic ingredients. However, their formulation is demanding due to stabil-
ity issues, which consequently affect the productsвАЩ quality.
Aims: We aimed to determine the quality (labeling accuracy, content determination, 
and content-¬≠related quality control) and stability under long-¬≠term and accelerated 
storage conditions of a representative sample of commercial cosmetics contain-
ing the most common B vitamins вАУ¬≠ nicotinamide, dexpanthenol, pyridoxine, and 
cyanocobalamin.
Methods: Cyanocobalamin was determined by a previously published stability-¬≠
indicating HPLCвАУ¬≠ diode array detector (DAD) method for the simultaneous determi-
nation of all hydrophilic vitamins. This method was additionally simplified and adjusted 
for the time-¬≠effective analysis of nicotinamide, dexpanthenol, and pyridoxine. Both 
methods were properly validated.
Results: All labeled B vitamins were present in the 36 tested products, mostly in con-
tents, reported effective on the skin. Thus, a straightforward correlation between 
vitamin contents and product prices were not observed. The content-¬≠related quality 
control of eight products, which quantitively specify their content, revealed signifi-
cantly lower nicotinamide contents (47% and 57%) in two products and appropriate 
or higher nicotinamide (102%вАУ¬≠112%) and dexpanthenol (100%вАУ¬≠104%) contents than 
declared in the remaining products. The 6-¬≠month long-¬≠term and accelerated stability 
studies demonstrated the productsвАЩ physical stability, but also revealed dexpanthenol, 
pyridoxine, and cyanocobalamin degradation, while nicotinamide was mostly stable in 
the tested products.
Conclusions: The obtained results provide an inside into the quality of commercial vi-
tamin B cosmetics and highlight the importance of stability testing in the formulation 
of quality, efficient, and safe cosmetics.

1вАГ |вАГ INTRODUCTION
B complex vitamins constitute a group of eight compounds includ-
ing thiamin, riboflavin, niacin, pantothenic acid, pyridoxine, biotin, 
folate, and cobalamin, with several specific functions in the human 
body. All B complex vitamins have also found their place in the 
cosmetic industry. While some are mostly used as excipients (e.g., 
riboflavin as colorant), others are established (nicotinamide, dexpan-
thenol, and biotin) or upcoming (thiamin, pyridoxine, folic acid, and 
cyanocobalamin) topical agents in dermatology and cosmetology 
with valuable effects on the skin.
Thiamin and riboflavin are used in the treatment of rosacea and 
seborrheic diseases, due to the antipruritic effect of thiamine and 
the anti-¬≠inflammatory effect of riboflavin.1 Because of the various 
beneficial effects on the skin, niacin in the form of nicotinamide 
is widely used as the (cosmetically) active ingredient in cosmetic 
and dermatological formulations. Nicotinamide has anti-¬≠microbial, 
anti-¬≠inflammatory, antipruritic, vasoactive, lightening, barrier-¬≠ and 
photo-¬≠protective, and sebostatic effects on the skin.2 Therefore, it 
is effective in the treatment of acne, atopic dermatitis, skin aging, 
melasma, light damage of the skin, hyperpigmentation, psoriasis, 
pellagra, nonmelanoma skin cancer, and other skin diseases and con-
ditions.2,3 Similarly, topical use of dexpanthenol, the alcohol analog 
of pantothenic acid, is widespread for the treatment and care of 
damaged skin, wound healing, atopic dermatitis, dermatitis, and scar 
management, due to its moisturizing, anti-¬≠inflammatory and effects 
on fibroblast proliferation.4 Pyridoxine has been recently found to 
specifically increase filaggrin transcription and production in the 
skin, making it a candidate for the treatment of atopic dermatitis.5 
Biotin acts as a hair and skin-¬≠conditioning (miscellaneous) agent and 
is thus commonly found in hair conditioners and masks as well as 
skincare products.1,6 Folic acid is an upcoming agent in the treat-
ment of photodamaged and aged skin due to its involvement in cell 
replication, regulation of gene activity, and skin renewal.7 The topical 
application of vitamin B12 is also a new approach in the treatment 
of atopic dermatitis and childhood eczema. Its beneficial effects are 
mediated through suppression of cytokine production, cell prolifera-
tion enhancement, and modulation of lymphocyte activity.8,9
Due to the many beneficial effects on the skin, B complex vita-
mins are common ingredients, added in different amounts in various 
cosmetic products, depending on their form and intended use. The 
main challenge associated with the formulation of B complex vita-
mins is assuring their stability. Most B complex vitamins are unstable 
and sensitive to various environmental factors (heat, oxygen, and 
light).10,11 Their instability in cosmetic products may lead to their 
degradation, which affects the quality and also efficacy, and safety 
of the product. The instability of B complex vitamins may also be as-
sociated with the formation of degradation products with different 
and unwanted effects on the skin. Typical examples include the hy-
drolysis of niacinamide and nicotinate esters to nicotinic acid, which 
induces intense skin reddening even at low concentrations,11 folic 
acid photodegradation to 6-¬≠formylpterin and pterin-¬≠6-¬≠carboxylic 
acid, which are associated with the generation of reactive oxygen 
species and skin photocarcinogenesis,12 and the photodegradation 
of pyridoxine to phototoxic products, associated with skin pho-
todamage and carcinogenesis.13
Cosmetic products on the EU market are not subject to regular 
quality control by the authorities, and their quality depends on the 
manufacturer. Cosmetics in the EU are regulated under Regulation EC 
1223/2009, which states that their sampling and analysis should be 
carried out in a reproducible and standardized manner to ensure the 
uniform application and control of the restrictions for substances,14 
whereas control of the active ingredients content and stability evalua-
tion are not required. Therefore, the growth of the cosmetic products 
market is accompanied by a growing concern regarding their quality by 
their users, some (accountable) manufacturers, and the scientific pub-
lic. The latter suggests stricter regulation and quality control, following 
the principles of the pharmaceutical industry and using an appropriate 
analytical methodology.15вАУ¬≠17 While there are some publications on 
the quality control and stability evaluation of lipophilic vitamins in 
commercial cosmetic products,15,16,18 such studies have not yet been 
reported for B complex vitamins. Considering their instability and its 
association with the quality, efficacy, and safety of cosmetic prod-
ucts, we aimed to determine the quality and stability of a represen-
tative sample of commercial leave-¬≠on cosmetic products containing 
the most common B complex vitaminsвАФ¬≠nicotinamide, dexpanthenol, 
pyridoxine, and cyanocobalamin. For such purposes, we used proper 
analytical methodology, based on conventional HPLCвАУ¬≠DAD systems.

The previously published stability-¬≠indicating HPLCвАУ¬≠DAD method

method of least squares was applied in regression analysis to deter-

The accuracy and precision of the methods were determined 
based on the QC solutions on three concentration levels, covering

a ratio (%) of the determined and actual concentration. The accep-
tance criterion was 100вАЙ¬±вАЙ5%. Precision was determined as a relative 
standard deviation (RSD) of the responses of the three parallels of

ity was also evaluated by six consecutive injections of one parallel of

The stability of the QC solutions was evaluated on each QC level

the response at time 0 and was expected to be within 100вАЙ¬±вАЙ10%.
Method recovery was determined individually for particular vita-

2.6вАГ |вАГ Commercial cosmetic products sample 
preparation
We developed the sample preparation procedure based on our ex-
perience and previously published procedures for the extraction of 
lipophilic vitamins.15,16 The optimal extraction solvent, its volume, 
and the amount of the weighted cosmetic product in the sample 
preparation procedure were determined within preliminary testing 
and were adjusted to each product. Samples from the obtained cos-
metic products were prepared in triplicate and analyzed immediately 
after their opening.
A predefined amount of the cosmetic products (25вАУ¬≠200вАЙmg) was 
initially weighted into a centrifuge tube, followed by the addition of 
5вАУ¬≠10¬†ml of Milli-¬≠Q water (in the case of liquid cosmetic products) 
or 10¬† ml of methanol or Milli-¬≠Q water (in the case of semi-¬≠solid 
cosmetic products). The samples were further vortexed (3¬† min), 
sonicated (15вАЙmin), vortexed once again (2¬† min), and centrifuged 
(16вАЙ200вАЙ√ЧвАЙg, 25¬∞C, 10¬†min). If needed, the samples were also filtered 
through an RC filter with a 0.45вАЙќЉm pore size and transferred into 
vials for analysis.
2.7вАГ |вАГ Stability study
The stability of selected cosmetic products was evaluated under ac-
celerated and long-¬≠term storage conditions in climate chambers, fol-
lowing the CTFA (Cosmetic, Toiletry, and Fragrance Association) and 
COLIPA (European Cosmetic Toiletry and Perfumery AssociationвАФ¬≠
now Cosmetic Europe) guidelines20 as well as the ICH Q1A (R2) 
guidelines.21 Long-¬≠term testing was performed on cosmetic products 
stored at 25¬∞C and 60% relative humidity (RH) in their original pack-
aging. Accelerated testing was performed by exposing previously

RH (40¬∞C and 75% RH). Changes in the tested vitamin contents and 
organoleptic properties of the cosmetic products were evaluated at 
different time points for 6¬†months: after 1¬†week, 2вАЙweeks, 3вАЙweeks, 1, 
and 6¬†months. Samples were prepared in triplicate at each time point

3вАГ |вАГ RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
3.1вАГ |вАГ Selection of analytes, commercial products, 
and analytical methods
The main aim of this study was to evaluate the content and stabil-
ity of frequently present B complex vitamins in commercial cos-
metic products and thus determine their quality. Therefore, we 
focused on leave-¬≠on cosmetic products, whose quality interrelates 
with their efficacy and safety due to their prolonged contact with 
the skin. Based on the performed screening of available leave-¬≠on 
cosmetic products on the Slovenian market, we concluded that 
nicotinamide and dexpanthenol are the main representatives of B 
complex vitamins in commercial cosmetics. Pyridoxine and cyano-
cobalamin are less often found, whereas other B complex vitamins 
(biotin and folic acid) are very rarely found in commercial cosmet-
ics on the Slovenian market. Therefore, such vitamin distribution 
is also maintained within our range of tested commercial cosmetic 
products, which included products in various price ranges and 
formulations in liquid and semi-¬≠solid forms from different manu-
facturers and also different products from the same manufactur-
ers. In view of the quality control of cosmetic products in relation 
to their declarations and labeling, products with declared vitamin 
content were also included in the study. For comparison purposes, 
representatives of B complex vitamins in similar formulations but 
in more strictly regulated productsвАФ¬≠medical devices and medici-
nal products were also included. The basis for content and sta-
bility evaluation is proper analytical methodology. A previously 
published stability-¬≠indicating HPLCвАУ¬≠DAD method for the simul-
taneous determination of all main hydrophilic vitamins10 was used 
for the evaluation of cyanocobalamin. This method was addition-
ally simplified and shortened resulting in an isocratic HPLCвАУ¬≠DAD 
method for the simultaneous quantification of nicotinamide, dex-
panthenol, and pyridoxine. The sample preparation procedure was 
developed and optimized for the individual cosmetic products.

guidelines Q2(R1) in terms of specificity, linearity, precision, injec-

The selected concentration ranges cover the evaluated vitaminsвАЩ

creams). Thereby, the determined nicotinamide contents gravitated 
toward three typical concentrations: 2.0%вАУ¬≠2.5% (w/w) (40% of the 
tested products), 4.2%вАУ¬≠4.6% (w/w) (27% of the tested products) and 
10.1%вАУ¬≠11.2% (w/w) (20% of the tested products). The majority (13) of 
the 15 tested cosmetic products contained nicotinamide in concentra-

skin appearance. Namely, the topical application of nicotinamide in 
concentrations between 2% and 5% (w/w) has been shown effective 
in the treatment of acne, and atopic dermatitis, and as a lightening, 
anti-¬≠aging, and anti-¬≠psoriatic agent.3,24 About 20% of the tested prod-

whose effects have not been investigated in the literature. However, 
clinical testing of nicotinamide in concentrations up to 10% (w/w) 
showed no stinging sensation.25 Also, as nicotinamide is not a sensi-
tizer or photosensitizer, it is considered safe in the current concen-
trations and practices of use, and its concentration is therefore not 
restricted in cosmetic products in the EU.25 Evaluating the relationship 
between nicotinamide content and the prices of the products, we did 
not observe a straightforward correlation (e.g., an increase in nicotin-
amide content with an increase in the products price). Although all 
products with high-¬≠nicotinamide content (ЋГ5% (w/w)) belonged to the 
mid or higher price range (15вАУ¬≠120вВђ per 50вАЙml of the cosmetic product), 
these price ranges also comprise products with some of the lowest 
determined nicotinamide contents (C24 and C32 in Figure¬†1).
The use of pyridoxine and cyanocobalamin in topical (cosmetic) 
products is upcoming and thus their effective concentrations have 
not yet been established. Also, they are quite rare ingredients in 
cosmetics products and their contents have not yet been reported. 
The determined pyridoxine content in the one tested product was 
0.557% (w/w). The determined cyanocobalamin content in the one 
tested cosmetic product was significantly lower (8.8вАЙ√ЧвАЙ10вИТ4% (w/w)).
3.4вАГ |вАГ B complex vitamins content-¬≠related quality 
control of the tested commercial products
Eight of the 36 evaluated commercial products (one medicinal prod-
uct and seven cosmetic products) quantitatively specified the content 
of nicotinamide (four products) and dexpanthenol (four products). 
With the increasing number of such products on the market and their 
insufficient control by the official authorities, there is also a growing 
need for their proper quality control, which was performed within 
this study. The content-¬≠related quality control was assessed by com-
paring the determined and the declared content (Figure¬†2).
As can be seen in Figure¬†2, all four tested cosmetic products con-
tained the declared dexpanthenol amount (100вАЙ¬±вАЙ5% of the declared 
content). Contrarily, the determined nicotinamide content was 
as stated (101.8% of the declared content) in one tested cosmetic 
product, higher than declared (112.2%) in one cosmetic product, 
and significantly lower (46.9% and 56.5%) in the remaining two cos-
metic products. Following the principles on acceptable tolerances 
for vitamins content in foods and food supplements, established by 
the European Commission based on their safety, on one hand, and 
general instability on the other,26 we consider the determined devi-
ations between the contained and declared vitamin amount in most 
products (C6, C12, C16, C18, C33, and MP1, Figure¬†2) acceptable. 
Contrary, as nicotinamide content significantly deviate from the la-
beled one in C14 and C32 (Figure¬†2), these two cosmetic products 
are inappropriate in view of their content-¬≠related quality. Although 
this is the first study, disclosing inappropriate vitamin B contents in 
relation to the declared contents in cosmetic products, such findings 
have been previously reported in food supplements.10,27 Possible

commercial products
To assess the overall stability of the tested commercial products 
and evaluate whether stability issues are a reason for the deter-
mined lower nicotinamide content in relation to the declared 
content in two cosmetic products, a stability study was performed 
following the CTFAвАУ¬≠COLIPA and ICH Q1A(R2) guidelines.20,21

22 products, including all products with quantitatively specified 
contents, except C16. The physical and chemical stability of the 
products was evaluated at regular time points during 6вАЙmonths of 
storage at long-¬≠term (25¬∞C and 60% RH) and accelerated stability

leptic properties were observed during the long-¬≠term and accel-

tested commercial products. Evaluating the chemical stability, we 
detected certain changes, which were specific to the individual vi-
tamins and formulations (Figure¬†3). Based on the obtained results,

Significant dexpanthenol degradation (>10% of the initial content)

the most common B complex vitamins in commercially available 
cosmetic products has not yet been described in the literature. 
However, the obtained results are supported by several reports 
on their stability in different samples (solutions, foods, and phar-
maceutical formulations) in the literature, where nicotinamide is 
ascertained as quite stable under various (stress) conditions, and 
dexpanthenol, pyridoxine, and cyanocobalamin as differently sus-

The observed higher degradation at elevated storage temperature 
is in line with the general principles and somewhat expected, as 
the temperature is one of the key environmental factors, affecting 
vitamin stability in general, although, contradictory information re-
garding its effect on the stability of dexpanthenol, pyridoxine, and 
cyanocobalamin may be found in the literature.10,28,29 Considering 
the inconsistent data on vitamins stability in the literature and their 
susceptibility to the effects of various factors, including also the 
formulation itself, stability studies should be applied as a useful 
tool in the formulation of quality cosmetic products. Especially ac-
celerated stability testing is gaining ground in the cosmetic indus-
try, as it may enable the prediction of the productвАЩs stability under 
the desired storage conditions in a shorter time and provides infor-
mation on the effect of short-¬≠term deviations from the expected 
storage conditions.21 However, prediction of the stability of prod-
ucts containing active ingredients and other excipients based on 
accelerated stability testing is quite demanding and has reported 
limited applicability to certain cosmetic products.18 Therefore, the 
results of accelerated stability testing should be confirmed by long-¬≠
term testing, as done in the pharmaceutical industry, and should 
include both determination of the physical and chemical stability 
of the products. This is also demonstrated by the obtained results 
within this study, where all products remained physically stable, 
whereas temperature had very different effects on the chemical 
stability of individual vitamins. Namely, temperature significantly 
affected B complex vitamins stability in some products (MD2, C9, 
C11, and C19, Figure¬†3), but did not have the (expected) effect on 
other products (C1, C11, and C33, Figure¬†3).
4вАГ |вАГ CONCLUSIONS
B complex vitamins have many favorable effects on the skin. 
Therefore, the topical application of nicotinamide and dexpanthenol 
is established in numerous skin conditions, while other B complex vi-
tamins (e.g., pyridoxine and cyanocobalamin) are forthcoming agents 
in cosmetology and dermatology. In view of their widespread use 
in the cosmetic industry, which is associated with some formulation 
challenges and the absence of regular quality control by the authori-
ties, we focused on the evaluation of the quality and stability of a 
representative sample of commercially available products with B 
complex vitamins. We thus evaluated the labeling accuracy, the con-
tent, and stability of the most prevalent B complex vitamins on the 
Slovenian cosmetic marketвАФ¬≠nicotinamide, dexpanthenol, pyridox-
ine, and cyanocobalamin in a total of 36 products. For such purpose, 
we used a previously developed HPLCвАУ¬≠DAD method, which was 
additionally simplified and shortened. The performed quality con-
trol confirmed the presence of all labeled B complex vitamins in the 
tested products but also revealed inappropriate contents in two of 
the eight tested cosmetics with declared vitamin B content. All the 
tested products were found physically stable after 6вАЙmonths of stor-
age at long-¬≠term and accelerated storage conditions. Nicotinamide 
was found stable in the tested products, while the stability of dex-
panthenol, pyridoxine, and cyanocobalamin depended on the for-
mulation and the storage temperature. Even though stability is a 
fundamental aspect in the formulation of quality cosmetic products, 
within this study we revealed significant dexpanthenol, pyridoxine, 
and cyanocobalamin degradation under both long-¬≠term and acceler-
ated stability conditions. Thus, with this article, we suggest a prin-
ciple for quality control and stability testing for both manufacturers 
and regulatory authorities and make an effort towards better rec-
ognition of the importance of stability for the production of quality 
and safe cosmetics.
